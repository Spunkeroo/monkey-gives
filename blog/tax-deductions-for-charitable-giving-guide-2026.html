Charitable giving is one of the most powerful tools in the tax code for reducing your tax burden while supporting causes you believe in. In 2026, the IRS allows taxpayers who itemize deductions to claim charitable contributions against their federal income tax. Done strategically, charitable giving can save you thousands of dollars in taxes while directing those funds to organizations making a positive impact.

This guide covers every aspect of charitable tax deductions as they apply in 2026, from basic rules to advanced strategies like bunching, Qualified Charitable Distributions, and donating appreciated assets. All information reflects current IRS rules and thresholds for the 2026 tax year.

Charitable Deduction Basics

To claim a charitable deduction, four conditions must be met:

  1. You itemize deductions on Schedule A of your federal income tax return (Form 1040). If you take the standard deduction, you cannot claim charitable deductions.
  2. You donate to a qualified organization. Only contributions to IRS-recognized 501(c)(3) organizations, churches, and certain other entities are deductible. Donations to individuals, political campaigns, and foreign charities (with limited exceptions) are not deductible.
  3. You receive nothing of value in return, or you reduce the deduction by the fair market value of any goods or services received. If a charity dinner costs $200 per ticket and the meal is worth $50, your deduction is $150.
  4. You have proper documentation. Requirements vary based on the amount and type of donation. Failing to maintain proper records can result in the IRS disallowing your entire deduction.

Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing in 2026

For the 2026 tax year, the standard deduction amounts are:

You should itemize only if your total itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction. Common itemized deductions include charitable contributions, state and local taxes (capped at $10,000), mortgage interest, and medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI. If your total is close to the standard deduction, the bunching strategy (described below) can push your deductions above the threshold in alternating years.

Important: TCJA Sunset in 2026

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) provisions are scheduled to expire after 2025. Congress may extend, modify, or allow them to expire. If the TCJA expires, the standard deduction will roughly halve, making itemizing beneficial for many more taxpayers. Watch for legislative updates that could significantly change the charitable giving landscape.

Deducting Cash Donations

Cash donations are the simplest form of charitable contribution. Cash includes currency, checks, credit card charges, electronic fund transfers, and online payments. Cash donations to public charities are deductible up to 60% of your adjusted gross income (AGI).

Example: If your AGI is $100,000, you can deduct up to $60,000 in cash donations to public charities. Any excess can be carried forward for up to five years.

Documentation for Cash Donations

  • Under $250: Bank record (canceled check, bank statement, credit card statement) or written receipt from the charity showing the organization name, date, and amount.
  • $250 and above: Written acknowledgment from the charity stating the amount donated, whether any goods or services were provided in exchange, and a description and good-faith estimate of the value of any goods or services received.

Deducting Stock and Securities

Donating appreciated stocks, bonds, or mutual fund shares held for more than one year is one of the most tax-efficient charitable giving strategies available. You receive two benefits:

  1. A charitable deduction for the full fair market value (FMV) of the securities at the time of donation.
  2. Complete avoidance of capital gains tax on the appreciation.

Example: You bought 100 shares of stock for $2,000 five years ago. The shares are now worth $10,000. If you sell and donate the cash, you pay approximately $1,904 in capital gains tax (20% + 3.8% NIIT on the $8,000 gain) and can donate the remaining $8,096. If you donate the shares directly, you avoid the $1,904 in tax, the charity receives the full $10,000, and your deduction is $10,000.

The deduction for appreciated securities is limited to 30% of AGI (rather than 60% for cash). Excess deductions carry forward for five years.

How to Donate Stock

  1. Contact the charity to get their brokerage account information (DTC number, account number).
  2. Contact your brokerage and initiate an in-kind transfer of shares to the charity's account.
  3. Do not sell the shares first. The tax benefit only applies to direct donations of the shares.
  4. The charity sells the shares (tax-free as a nonprofit) and uses the proceeds.
  5. Request a donation acknowledgment letter from the charity for your records.

Deducting Cryptocurrency Donations

The IRS treats cryptocurrency as property for tax purposes, which means donating crypto follows the same rules as donating other appreciated property. For crypto held more than one year, you can deduct the fair market value at the time of donation and avoid capital gains tax on the appreciation.

For crypto held one year or less, your deduction is limited to your cost basis (what you paid for it), not the current market value. You still avoid capital gains tax, but the deduction is less favorable.

Special Rule: No Wash Sale for Crypto

Unlike stocks, cryptocurrency is not subject to the wash sale rule as of 2026. This means you can donate appreciated crypto, claim the full FMV deduction, avoid capital gains tax, and then immediately repurchase the same cryptocurrency with cash. Your new cost basis resets to the current market price. This strategy is sometimes called "donation harvesting" and is perfectly legal under current IRS rules.

Appraisal Requirement for Crypto Over $5,000

For cryptocurrency donations valued over $5,000, the IRS requires a qualified appraisal by a qualified appraiser within 60 days of the donation. You must also file Form 8283, Section B with your tax return. Some crypto donation platforms like The Giving Block provide valuation documentation that may satisfy this requirement, but consult your tax advisor.

Deducting Property and Goods

Donations of tangible personal property (clothing, furniture, vehicles, art, etc.) are deductible at their fair market value, subject to certain rules:

  • Clothing and household items must be in good used condition or better to be deductible. The deduction is for the thrift-store or resale value, not the original purchase price.
  • Vehicles donated to charity are subject to special rules. If the charity sells the vehicle, your deduction is limited to the gross proceeds of the sale (you will receive a Form 1098-C). If the charity uses the vehicle in its programs, you can deduct the fair market value.
  • Art and collectibles valued over $5,000 require a qualified appraisal. If the charity uses the art in a way related to its exempt purpose (e.g., a museum displays it), you can deduct the full FMV. If the charity sells it, your deduction may be limited to your cost basis.

AGI Limitation Rules

The IRS limits how much you can deduct in a single year based on the type of donation and the type of recipient organization:

Donation TypePublic CharityPrivate Foundation
Cash60% of AGI30% of AGI
Appreciated assets (held 1+ year)30% of AGI20% of AGI
Short-term assets (held 1 year or less)50% of AGI30% of AGI

When multiple limits apply, the IRS has ordering rules. The 60% limit is reduced by the amount of 30% contributions, and so on. Amounts exceeding the limit carry forward for up to five years, subject to the same percentage limitations each year.

Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCD)

If you are 70.5 years old or older, a Qualified Charitable Distribution from your IRA is one of the most tax-efficient giving strategies available. A QCD allows you to transfer up to $105,000 per year (2026 limit, adjusted annually for inflation) directly from your IRA to a qualified charity.

Benefits of a QCD:

  • The distribution is excluded from your taxable income entirely (not just a deduction, but full exclusion).
  • It counts toward your Required Minimum Distribution (RMD) if you are 73 or older.
  • It works even if you take the standard deduction instead of itemizing.
  • It can reduce your AGI, which may lower Medicare premiums, reduce Social Security taxation, and affect other income-based thresholds.

To qualify, the distribution must go directly from your IRA trustee to the charity. You cannot withdraw the funds first and then donate them. Contact your IRA custodian to set up a QCD.

Donor-Advised Funds

A Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) is a charitable giving vehicle that allows you to make a contribution, receive an immediate tax deduction, and then recommend grants to charities over time. Think of it as a charitable savings account.

How DAFs Work

  1. You contribute cash, stocks, crypto, or other assets to your DAF account at a sponsoring organization (Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, Vanguard Charitable, etc.).
  2. You receive an immediate tax deduction for the full amount contributed in the year of contribution.
  3. The funds are invested and grow tax-free within the DAF.
  4. You recommend grants to specific charities whenever you choose. There is no deadline to distribute funds.

DAF Advantages

  • Timing flexibility: Claim the deduction now, distribute to charities later.
  • Bunching enabler: Contribute multiple years of donations in one year to exceed the standard deduction, then distribute grants annually.
  • Tax-free growth: Invested funds grow without capital gains or income tax.
  • Simplified record-keeping: One contribution, one tax receipt. The DAF handles receipts for individual grants.
  • Low minimums: Fidelity Charitable requires just $5,000 to open an account.

The Bunching Strategy

Bunching (also called "lumping") is a strategy for taxpayers whose annual charitable giving alone does not push their total itemized deductions above the standard deduction. Instead of donating the same amount every year, you concentrate two or more years of donations into a single year.

Bunching Example

A married couple with $20,000 in annual state/local taxes (capped at $10,000 for federal deduction) and $12,000 in mortgage interest has $22,000 in non-charitable itemized deductions. The 2026 standard deduction for married filing jointly is $31,400.

StrategyYear 1 DeductionYear 2 DeductionTotal 2-Year Benefit
Give $5,000/year (standard both years)$31,400 (standard)$31,400 (standard)$62,800
Bunch: $10,000 Year 1, $0 Year 2$32,000 (itemize)$31,400 (standard)$63,400

By bunching, the couple gains an additional $600 in deductions over two years. For higher-income taxpayers with larger charitable budgets, the savings can be much more significant. Pairing the bunching strategy with a DAF allows you to maintain consistent annual giving to your chosen charities while optimizing your tax deductions.

Deductions You Cannot Claim

Several common misconceptions about charitable deductions lead to errors on tax returns:

  • Value of volunteer time: You cannot deduct the value of your time or services, regardless of your hourly rate or expertise. You can deduct unreimbursed expenses incurred while volunteering (mileage at 14 cents/mile, supplies, travel).
  • Donations to individuals: Giving money directly to a person in need, even through a GoFundMe campaign, is not tax deductible. Only donations to qualified organizations count.
  • Political contributions: Donations to political parties, campaigns, or PACs are never tax deductible.
  • Raffle tickets and lottery: The cost of raffle tickets, charity auctions where you receive fair market value, or other gambling-related donations are not deductible as charitable contributions.
  • Blood donations: You cannot deduct the value of blood donated to the Red Cross or blood banks.
  • Foreign charities: Donations to foreign charities are generally not deductible unless the charity has US 501(c)(3) status or falls under a specific tax treaty provision.
  • Tuition or fees disguised as donations: Payments to schools, churches, or organizations that are essentially tuition or fees for services are not deductible as charitable contributions.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much can I deduct for charitable donations in 2026?

For cash donations to public charities, you can deduct up to 60% of your adjusted gross income (AGI). For appreciated assets like stocks or crypto held over one year, the limit is 30% of AGI. For donations to private foundations, the limit is 30% for cash and 20% for appreciated assets. Excess deductions can be carried forward for up to five years.

Do I need to itemize to deduct charitable donations?

Yes, as of 2026, you must itemize deductions on Schedule A to claim charitable contribution deductions. The temporary above-the-line deduction for non-itemizers that was available during 2020-2021 has expired. If your total itemized deductions do not exceed the standard deduction ($15,700 single, $31,400 married filing jointly in 2026), itemizing does not provide a tax benefit. Consider the bunching strategy or QCDs as alternatives.

Can I deduct the value of my volunteer time?

No, the IRS does not allow deductions for the value of time or services donated to charity, regardless of your professional rate. However, you can deduct unreimbursed out-of-pocket expenses incurred while volunteering, such as mileage (14 cents per mile for charitable purposes in 2026), supplies you purchase for the organization, and travel expenses directly related to your volunteer work.

What documentation do I need for charitable deductions?

For cash donations under $250, a bank record or receipt is sufficient. For donations of $250 or more, you need a written acknowledgment from the charity before you file your return. For non-cash donations over $500, you must file Form 8283. For non-cash donations over $5,000, a qualified appraisal is required within 60 days of the donation. Keep all documentation for at least three years after filing.

Is it better to donate cash or stock to charity?

Donating appreciated stock or other assets held more than one year is generally more tax-efficient than donating cash. You avoid paying capital gains tax on the appreciation (saving 20-23.8% of the gain) and still receive a deduction for the full fair market value. The only downside is the lower AGI limit (30% vs. 60%). If your donations are a small percentage of your AGI, appreciated assets are almost always the better choice.
